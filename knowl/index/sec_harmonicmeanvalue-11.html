We finish our excursion about harmonic functions with a preview and its consequences. We say a real valued function \(u\) on a region \(G\) has a weak relative maximum at \(w\) if there exists a disk \(D[w,r] \subseteq G\) such that all \(z \in D[w,r]\) satisfy \(u(z) \leq u(w)\text{.}\) We define weak relative minimum similarly. In [cross-reference to target(s) "taylorchapter" missing or not unique] we will strengthen Theorem 6.2.3 and Corollary 6.2.4 to [cross-reference to target(s) "maxmodthm" missing or not unique] and [cross-reference to target(s) "maxminharmcor" missing or not unique] by replacing strong relative extremum in the hypotheses with weak relative extremum.
 1 
In particular, we will show that one does not have to assume that \(f\) is nonzero in a region \(G\) to have a strong relative maximum in \(G\text{.}\)
A special but important case of the maximum/minimum principle for harmonic functions, [cross-reference to target(s) "maxminharmcor" missing or not unique], concerns bounded regions. In [cross-reference to target(s) "taylorchapter" missing or not unique] we will establish that, if \(u\) is harmonic in a bounded region \(G\) and continuous on its closure, then
\begin{equation} \sup_{z \in G} u(z) \= \max_{z \in \partial G} u(z) \qquad \text{ and } \qquad \inf_{z \in G} u(z) \= \min_{z \in \partial G} u(z)\tag{6.1} \end{equation}
where, as usual, \(\partial G\) denotes the boundary of \(G\text{.}\) We’ll exploit this in the next two corollaries.
